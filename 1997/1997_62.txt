                        DWARF GALAXIES AND COSMOLOGY


I. Scientific Rationale

Dwarf galaxies are the most numerous type of galaxies in the Universe and
are the "building blocks" of giant galaxies in the paradigm of the
hierarchical growth of structures. They are central to many current issues
in extragalactic astronomy and cosmology. This meeting wishes to compare
the individual and statistical  properties of local dwarf galaxies with
those of dwarfs at large (z about 1-2) redshifts and discuss the
evolution of these building blocks as a function of look-back time.

We are currently seeing a surge of activity in the study of dwarf galaxies,
driven in part by their scientific interest, but also by the availability
of new technology : wide field imaging telescopes, CCD detectors and
sensitive spectrographs. This has brought and will continue to bring major
advances in our knowledge of dwarf galaxies. This meeting which is being
held 4.5 years after the previous meeting on dwarf galaxies at the
Observatoire de Haute Provence, will be most opportune for summarizing and
assessing the progress made in the study of dwarf galaxies and their impact
on cosmological issues and chart out new directions for the future, both
observationally and theoretically.

The scientific issues which will be discussed in the meeting are :

- The statistical properties of local dwarf galaxies.

There are now several optical surveys using wide-field cameras with
objective prisms or Ha filters coupled with CCDs to search for local new
star forming dwarfs (also called blue compact dwarf galaxies or BCDs).
There are also ongoing or future surveys in other wawelengths to search for
HI intergalactic clouds and low-surface-brightness (LSB) dwarfs : blind
sensitive HI searches, deep infrared searches (2-MASS, ISO) and UV surveys
(ASTRO, FUSE).

We shall discuss the faint end of the optical luminosity function of
galaxies both in the field and in clusters of galaxies. The H a surveys
provide the star-formation rate (SFR) at zero redshift, which can be
compared with the SFR at higher redshifts, to study the SFR as a function
of look-back time. The HI mass function provides important contraints on
the population of LSB galaxies and is an important input into models of
cosmology and galaxy formation.

- Intrinsic properties of nearby young dwarf galaxies relevant to cosmology.

We shall discuss the properties of the most metal deficient blue compact
dwarf galaxies known. These are among the least chemically evolved galaxies
known in the Universe, their metal content being between 1/50 and 1/3 that
of the Sun.
They thus best approximate primeval galaxies and constitute excellent
nearby laboratories for studying star formation in nearly pristine gas. We
shall discuss the intrinsic properties of dwarf galaxies which are most
relevant to cosmological studies.


The topics to be covered are :

a) The Lyman alpha emission and absorption in BCDs.

The absence of Lya emission in high-redshift galaxies is reminiscent of the
behavior of Lya emission in nearby BCD galaxies, which is either absent or
greatly diminished. The study of nearby young galaxies can shed light on
the lack of high z Lya-emitting galaxies.

b) HI primordial envelopes.

The young nearby dwarf galaxies appear to be surrounded by nearly
primordial HI envelopes which may be responsible for absorbing the Lya
emission of the star-forming regions. What has prevented these HI clouds
from collapsing and forming stars before the present starburst ?

c) Interaction of massive star formation with the Interstellar Medium.

In star-forming dwarf galaxies, the numerous massive stars made during the
starburst episodes can cause significant damage to their surrounding
interstellar medium through UV emission, stellar winds and supernova
explosions, resulting in the production of shells and supershells, or
blow-outs.

d) Connection between BCDs, dIs and dEs.

Star formation in BCDs cannot be continuous but occurs by bursts lasting
less than 10 million years and separated by several billion years. LSB
dwarfs (also called dwarf irregulars or dIs) are generally thought to be
BCDs in their quiescent stage. Dwarf elliptical (dEs) have on the other
hand little HI gas and star formation. What are the evolutionary links
between the 3 types of dwarf galaxies ?

e) Dark matter.

Finally, there is the problem of dark matter in dwarf galaxies. There
appears to be general agreement that dEs contain a large amount of dark
matter, but there is still considerable debate concerning dark matter in
dIs and BCDs. This question is very important for galaxy formation theory
as dwarf galaxies are considered to be the "building blocks" of giant
galaxies and the latter are known to contain dark matter.


- Dwarf galaxies in deep redshift surveys

We shall discuss the properties of the dwarf galaxies at high redshift
discovered in deep optical and near-infrared surveys. The surveys include
the Hubble Deep Field with follow-up spectroscopic surveys with the Keck
telescope, and surveys carried out at the CFHT (the CFRS survey) and AAT
telescopes.

We shall also discuss the damped Lya systems seen in the direction of
quasars and compare their properties with those of the HI envelopes seen
around local BCDs.


- Models and theory

We shall discuss dwarf galaxy formation theory, focussing on the triggering
mechanisms of the starburst and the interaction of the star forming region
with the surrounding interstellar medium (stellar winds, chemical
evolution). We shall also address the constraints that nearly primordial HI
envelopes seen around dwarf galaxies can put on the existence of population
III stars.

Deep galaxy counts and redshift surveys have revealed a dominant population
of star-forming dwarf galaxies with redshifts of about 1-2. A large dwarf
population is also expected from simulations of galaxy evolution based on
the cold dark matter model. However, the theory predicts for more dwarfs
than are observed locally  until now. How do the results of the new dwarf
surveys compare with theoritical predictions ? What constraints can be put
on cosmological models from the local observed space density of dwarf
galaxies ?

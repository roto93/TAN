MOTIVATION FOR 'THE RESTLESS UNIVERSE' 
The gravitational N-body problem dominates much of theoretical
astrophysics. It arises in problems ranging from the motion of
artificial and natural satellites in the Solar System, to the formation
and evolution of large-scale structure in the Universe. On intermediate
scales the N-body dynamics of  gravitational systems dominates the
behaviour of stars in clusters and galaxies. 

Study of the gravitational N-body problem is a very old, and therefore
very mature, subject. The fact that it was the starting point for the
work of scientists of the stature of Newton, Lagrange, Poincare, Henon,
and many others, proves its seminal importance for the whole of physics
- an importance which continues to the present day. 

Since the early years of the present century the techniques and
scientific issues involved in this problem have diversified widely, even
within the field of astrophysics alone.  As a result, developments in
these subdisciplines have proceeded independently. Celestial mechanics
was sometimes in danger of losing contact all together with the rest of
astronomy. In stellar dynamics, theorists went through a period of
rediscovering what had long been familiar in celestial mechanics.
Recently there have been signs of renewed cross-fertilisation between
these disciplines.  For example, techniques for the study of nearly
periodic motions of the planets have been taken up in stellar dynamics,
while at the same time the discovery of extrasolar planetary systems has
given the general astrophysical community a renewed appetite for the
kinds of techniques that were common in celestial mechanics. 

AIM
The aim of the `The Restless Universe' is to stimulate the
cross-fertilisation of ideas, methods and applications between the
different communities who work in the gravitational N-body problem,
across diverse fields of astrophysics. The lectures and topics of the
Institute will cover three broad themes: the dynamics of the solar
system, the dynamics of galaxies and star clusters, and the large scale
structure of the Universe.

FORMAT
Mornings and early afternoons will be devoted to the courses by invited
lecturers.  Most evenings will be occupied by special invited addresses
and discussions on related topics.  The afternoons are reserved for
presentations by participants in the Institute. In addition, informal
activities, contacts and discussions will average, from previous
experience, several additional hours per day. This ASI will bring
together many of the brightest of our young people working in galactic,
stellar and planetary dynamical astronomy, enabling them to obtain an
up-to-date synoptic view of their subjects delivered by lecturers of
high international reputation.  A book of the ASI will be published as
soon as possible after the ASI closes.

LOCATION
The NATO ASI will be held in a four star hotel in the heart of the
spectacular mountain scenery of the Scottish Highlands.  Built in 1832,
the Atholl Arms Hotel is completely refurbished in the style of a
traditional highland hotel, offering a variety of outdoor pursuits and
cultural interests.  It is located in the village of Blair Atholl,
situated in Perthshire, approximately 100 miles (160 km) north of
Glasgow and Edinburgh.

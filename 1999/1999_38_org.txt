3. SCIENTIFIC RATIONALE FOR THE SYMPOSIUM: The frequency window available
=09to ground based radio astronomers for exploring the Universe
=09extends all the way from about 20 MHz to 100 GHz. Although much of
=09the early pioneering observations and discoveries in radio
=09astronomy were made at the low frequency end (less than ~1 GHz) of
=09the window, there has been a steady shift in emphasis with time
=09towards higher frequencies for various reasons, the most important
=09of which, was the quest for higher and higher angular resolution.
=09This has resulted in the relative neglect of the low frequency
=09region even though there are many astrophysical problems and
=09phenomena that are best studied at these longer wavelengths.
=09Additional factors which contributed to this situation were
=09technical difficulties associated with low frequency aperture
=09synthesis, such as the need for ionospheric phase correction over
=09large fields of view of the individual array elements, and the
=09need for a 3-D inversion of the visibilities. With the dramatic
=09improvement in the availability of affordable computing power,
=09these problems are now more tractable.=20

=09A few instruments, such as the Very Large Array (VLA), Westerbork
=09Synthesis Radio Telescope (WSRT), Molonglo Synthesis Telescope
=09(MOST) and Ooty Radio Telescope (ORT) have been making significant
=09contributions at frequencies below ~1 GHz and the new developments=20
=09at some of these observatories should make it possible very soon to
=09considerably strengthen the low-frequency capabilities. While the
=09lowest frequency of operation of the VLA so far was 327 MHz, the
=09system has recently been outfitted to observe also at the much
=09lower frequency of 74 MHz and the first observing runs at this
=09frequency have proved to be very successful.  The wide band feeds
=09on the WSRT antennas would soon provide a near continuous
=09frequency coverage between about 250 MHz and 1.4 GHz for HI
=09studies. In the southern hemisphere, MOST operating at 843 MHz is
=09just commencing one of the most sensitive radio surveys ever
=09undertaken, and a new radio telescope in Mauritius in the southern
=09hemisphere has recently become operational at 150 MHz. Perhaps the
=09most important new development is the Giant Metrewave Radio
=09Telescope (GMRT) near Pune in India which, with its 30 parabolic
=09dishes of 45m diameter each, is the most powerful radio telescope
=09at longer wavelengths operating at a number of radio frequencies
=09between about 150 MHz and 1.4 GHz. Now coming into operation,
=09GMRT can be expected to make a number of important contributions=20
=09in different areas of metre-wavelength radio astronomy.

=09Considering these important developments, an IAU Symposium on
=09``The Universe at Low Radio Frequencies'' in Pune, India, should
=09be scientifically quite fruitful. In addition to reviewing and
=09discussing recent new results in a number of astrophysical areas,
=09the symposium will also address the special instrumental and other
=09problems associated with doing radio astronomy at low radio
=09frequencies, such as wide field mapping, ionospheric effects,
=09interference excision, etc. It would also serve to focus attention
=09on the technical aspects of the future Square Kilometre Array
=09project and the science that it would make possible.
